# Scoring Framework

How to weigh keyword candidates against each other when the data points in different directions.

## The Core Frame

Six factors. The first two carry the most weight.

| Factor | Weight | How to read it |
|---|---|---|
| Intent match to business goal | High | Does this term, ranked, drive the outcome the client wants? |
| Realistic difficulty for this site | High | Can this site plausibly rank for this in 6-12 months? |
| Search volume | Medium | More demand is better, but only after the first two pass |
| Current proximity to page 1 | Medium | Terms on page 2 are the cheapest wins |
| SERP feature opportunity | Medium | Can the page capture a featured snippet, PAA slot, AI Overview citation, local pack spot? |
| Strategic value | Variable | Brand defense, category authority, future cluster anchor |

## How to Apply the Frame

There is no clean formula. The factors trade off against each other, and the right trade depends on the client. A few patterns that come up often:

### Pattern: High volume, high difficulty, perfect intent

Example: a roofer chasing "roof replacement" (volume 50,000+, KD 80+, transactional intent).

This is a sucker bet for a small site. The intent and volume look great, but the difficulty makes the ROI horrible. Three years of work might never break page 1.

Better: chase the long-tail variant the same searcher uses next. "roof replacement [neighborhood]" (volume 200, KD 25, transactional) is winnable, and the lifetime value of one closed roof job is huge.

### Pattern: Low volume, low difficulty, perfect intent

Example: "asphalt shingle replacement greenwood indiana" (volume 30, KD 12, transactional).

Easy to dismiss. Do not dismiss. For a local service business with high-value transactions, 30 monthly searches is 360 a year, with a closing rate of maybe 5%, equals 18 jobs a year, at $15,000 average ticket. That is one keyword.

Score these high if the business model can monetize them. Score them low if the LTV does not justify.

### Pattern: High volume, low difficulty, weak intent

Example: a roofer considering "best roof shingles 2026" (volume 5,000, KD 22, informational).

Tempting because the numbers look good. But the searcher is not in the buying journey. Even if the page ranks #1, conversion is low.

Decide based on funnel logic: if the client has a working middle-of-funnel and bottom-of-funnel, top-of-funnel informational content is a fine investment in topical authority. If they do not yet have the converting content in place, build that first.

### Pattern: Page 2 ranking, decent intent, decent volume

Example: client already ranks #14 for "roof replacement cost indianapolis" (volume 480, KD 35, commercial investigation).

Almost always Tier 1. The site has already done the hard part: getting on the SERP at all. Push to page 1 with title rewrite, content expansion, internal links, schema. ROI per hour of work is among the best available.

Always pull GSC and surface page 2 rankings as candidates before doing greenfield research.

## Tiering Decisions

The output of scoring is a tier assignment. Three tiers:

- **Tier 1: Build now.** 10-20 keywords. Each gets a dedicated page or claims an existing page. Drives the next quarter of work.
- **Tier 2: Build next.** 20-40 keywords. Queued for the following quarter.
- **Tier 3: Watch.** Everything else. Useful as secondary terms in content briefs, future expansion candidates, signal for monitoring.

A keyword belongs in Tier 1 if YES to all of these:

- Intent matches a business outcome the client cares about
- Difficulty is realistic for the site within 6-12 months
- Volume justifies the build effort (with caveats for high-LTV local services)
- A page exists or can be built to own the term

If one of these fails, drop to Tier 2 or Tier 3.

## Worked Trade-Off Examples

### Trade-off 1: A higher-volume term vs a more on-intent term

- Term A: "roofer indianapolis" (volume 1,900, KD 42, transactional)
- Term B: "roof replacement indianapolis" (volume 1,300, KD 38, transactional)

Both pass the intent and difficulty bar. Term A has more volume. Term B has tighter intent (the customer has already decided it is a replacement, not a repair, and is looking for a contractor). Term B closes at a higher rate.

Choice: both go in Tier 1. They point to the same general service area and the site can target both with one page or two related pages.

### Trade-off 2: A high-volume informational term vs a low-volume commercial term

- Term C: "how to know if you need a new roof" (volume 3,600, KD 18, informational)
- Term D: "free roof inspection indianapolis" (volume 90, KD 24, transactional)

Term C is appealing because the volume is large and the difficulty is low. But the searcher is a year away from buying.

Term D is small in volume but the searcher is calling someone this week.

Choice: depends on the funnel state. If the site has the basics covered and is investing in topical authority, Term C is a good Tier 1. If the site needs leads in the next 90 days, Term D is the Tier 1, and Term C waits.

### Trade-off 3: A term the site already ranks for vs a fresh opportunity

- Term E: site currently #11 for "roof replacement cost" (volume 1,200, KD 45, commercial)
- Term F: site does not rank for "metal roof installation indianapolis" (volume 240, KD 28, transactional)

Term E is a page-2 push. Six hours of work to rewrite the title and expand the cost section could move it from #11 to #6. The traffic gain is immediate.

Term F is greenfield. A new page, a writer's day to produce, a few weeks for Google to discover and rank.

Choice: Term E is the lower-risk, faster-payback play. Do it first. Term F is a strong Tier 1 follow-up.

## When to Override the Frame

The strategic value column exists because some terms are worth pursuing for reasons the scoring frame cannot capture:

- **Brand defense.** If a competitor is ranking #1 for the client's brand name plus modifier ("[brand] reviews," "[brand] vs [competitor]"), the client should own that SERP regardless of volume or difficulty.
- **Category authority anchor.** Some informational pieces define a category. Owning "what is helpful content" makes a site visible to everyone learning the term. Worth pursuing even at unfavorable scores if the brand wants to be the source.
- **AI citation play.** Some terms are unlikely to drive much clicks-from-Google traffic but get cited heavily in AI Overviews and Reddit threads. Worth pursuing for AEO programs.
- **Service launch.** If the client is launching a new service, the keywords supporting it move to Tier 1 regardless of current scoring data, because the page needs to exist.

Strategic-value overrides should be flagged in the output so the rationale is clear to whoever inherits the work.
