# Reviews Playbook

How to build a sustainable Google review velocity program. The single highest-leverage GBP optimization most local businesses neglect.

## Why Reviews Matter So Much

Three reasons in order of weight:

1. **Local pack ranking signal.** Reviews are a major prominence factor. Volume, recency, and rating all feed into the local pack algorithm.
2. **Conversion signal.** Customers comparing local options choose by review count and star average before they choose by website quality. A 4.8-star 200-review business beats a 4.5-star 30-review competitor most of the time.
3. **Trust signal at scale.** Recent reviews tell customers and Google the business is active and responsive. A profile with the last review from 2023 reads as defunct.

## Targets by Business Stage

- **New / small business (under 50 total reviews):** Target 4-8 new reviews per month. Goal: reach 50 total within a year, 100 within two years.
- **Established business (50-200 reviews):** Target 4-10 new reviews per month. Goal: maintain steady cadence.
- **Mature business (200+ reviews):** Target 5+ per month minimum. The marginal value per review decreases, but velocity still matters for Google's "active business" signal.

These targets assume legitimate review-request workflows. Sudden spikes (10+ reviews in a week followed by silence) pattern-match to campaigns and risk Google filtering them.

## The Request System

Three components: timing, channel, message.

### Timing

Ask at peak satisfaction. For most service businesses, that is the same day or the day after the work is complete and the customer is happy.

- **Service business:** within 24 hours of job completion, after a final walkthrough or confirmation that the customer is satisfied.
- **Restaurant / retail:** within hours of the visit, while the experience is fresh. Often via tabletop card, receipt link, or follow-up text if the customer opted in.
- **B2B service:** at a defined milestone (project kickoff success, first quarterly review, contract renewal) rather than at random.

### Channel

The channel that already works with the customer.

- **SMS** has the highest response rate. Requires opt-in.
- **Email** works but converts lower. Useful when SMS is not available.
- **In-person** at point of service still works for retail and restaurants. A staff member politely mentioning "If you have a moment, a Google review really helps us out" is fine.
- **QR code on receipts or invoices** for high-volume low-touch businesses.

Avoid:

- Cold calls asking for reviews.
- Public posts on the customer's social media tagging them and asking publicly.

### Message

Short, polite, makes it easy. Avoid begging, avoid demanding, avoid implying a specific outcome.

Template SMS:

```
Hi [name], [staff name] from [business]. Hope everything is working great after [service / visit]. If you have a moment, would you mind leaving us a Google review? It really helps a small local business like ours: [direct link to review form]. Thank you either way!
```

Template email subject: `Quick favor from [business]`

Template email body:

```
Hi [name],

Hope you're happy with [the work we did / your recent visit / your purchase].

If you have a couple of minutes, a Google review would mean a lot. Here's the direct link:

[Link to review form]

If anything wasn't right, please reach out to me directly first: [phone] / [email]. We'd rather fix it than read about it later.

Thanks for choosing us.

[Name]
[Business]
```

Notes on the templates:

- **Always make the review link a direct deep link to the review form.** Use the GBP "Place ID" lookup tool to generate a `g.page/r/...` shortlink that opens straight to the review modal.
- **The opt-out for unhappy customers ("please reach out to me directly first if anything wasn't right") is critical.** It catches issues before they become public reviews. It is also legal and ethical: you are not gating reviews, you are offering a service recovery path that the customer is free to ignore.
- **Avoid wording that implies a 5-star expectation.** "Hope you'll give us 5 stars" violates Google's review policy by influencing the rating. The customer decides the rating; you ask for the review.

## Review Gating (Do Not Do It)

Some businesses screen customers before sending review requests (e.g., asking "how satisfied are you?" and only sending the review link to those who answer 5/5). This is called review gating. Google's terms explicitly prohibit it. Detected gating leads to review removal and can lead to profile suspension.

The legitimate version of this concept: ask every relevant customer, with a service-recovery option for unhappy ones. The customer chooses whether to leave the review. This is allowed and standard.

## Responses

Respond to every review. Yes, even the bad ones. Especially the bad ones.

### Positive review response

- Open with a thank-you that mentions the customer's specific point ("Thanks so much, Jamie. Glad the crew finished a day early and that the cleanup was so clean.").
- Add one personal or contextual detail (the project, the team member, the location served).
- Close with a warm note. Avoid asking for referrals; the customer just gave you a review.

Length: 2-4 sentences.

### Negative review response

- Open with acknowledgment, not defense. ("Thanks for the feedback, [name]. I'm sorry the experience didn't meet our standards.")
- Take responsibility where appropriate. Do not blame the customer in writing.
- Offer a specific service recovery path. ("I'd like to make this right. Please call me directly at [phone].")
- Keep it brief. Do not litigate the dispute in public.

Length: 3-5 sentences max.

If a review is factually false (the reviewer was never a customer), report via Google's review removal process. Document evidence. Removal is slower and less reliable than people hope, but for clearly fake reviews it sometimes works.

### Response time

- Within 24-48 hours of the review posting. Most owners are notified by email; ensure that notification reaches the right person.
- Google tracks response activity. Frequent unanswered reviews signal an inactive profile.

## Handling a Critical Mass of Negative Reviews

If the profile has 3+ negative reviews in a short window, something real may be happening:

- A service issue that needs fixing at the operations level, not the response level.
- A bad-faith competitor or disgruntled former employee.
- A misunderstanding about expectations that needs to be addressed in marketing materials.

Respond to each professionally, but then take the diagnostic question seriously offline. The profile cannot be "fixed" by responses alone if the underlying experience is the problem.

## Review Velocity Maintenance

Once a request system is in place, monitor monthly:

- **Requests sent:** track the count.
- **Reviews received:** track the count.
- **Conversion rate:** reviews per 100 requests is a useful benchmark. Most service businesses land at 10-25%.
- **Star average trend:** any drop investigated.
- **Specific reviewer themes:** mining patterns in the language (positive and negative) feeds into marketing, product, and operations conversations.

When velocity slows, audit the system. Is the request firing for every job? Is the link working? Has the timing slipped? Most velocity problems are workflow problems, not customer problems.

## Multi-Location Considerations

For multi-location businesses, each location has its own profile and its own reviews. Track per location. Local managers should own their location's review responses; corporate can set guidelines but should not centralize responses (slow, generic, ineffective).

## Integration With Operations

The most successful review programs are integrated into operations, not bolted on as marketing.

- A field service tech triggers the review request from the job-complete confirmation in the dispatch software.
- A point-of-sale system sends the review request after a transaction completes.
- A B2B account manager has the review ask as part of the project-close playbook.

Reviews as a project marketing checks weekly are workable. Reviews as a habit baked into the work itself produce the best long-term velocity.

## What Not to Do

- **Do not pay for reviews.** Includes "incentives" for honest reviews (discounts, gift cards, raffle entries). Violates Google's policy.
- **Do not write reviews from staff accounts.** Detected; deleted; sometimes leads to profile suspension.
- **Do not buy fake reviews from review-for-hire services.** Increasingly detectable and increasingly penalized.
- **Do not screen for star rating before sending the request.** Gating; not allowed.
- **Do not respond to negative reviews with personal information about the reviewer** (their full name if they posted with initials only, their service history, their address). Privacy violation; bad-faith.
- **Do not delete or hide your own profile after a bad review.** Reads as worse than the review.

A clean, consistent, legitimate review program over 12 months outperforms any quick-fix tactic by a wide margin. There are no real shortcuts here.
