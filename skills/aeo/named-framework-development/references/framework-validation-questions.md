# Framework Validation Questions

The interview and validation questions to use during Stage 1 (finding the insight) and the naming-test phase of Stage 2. Use these as starting points; adapt to the brand's category.

## Stage 1 Interview Questions

For the brand's senior practitioners. Aim for 60-90 minutes of conversation per interview. Record (with permission); the verbatim insights often become the framework's foundation.

### Opening Questions (set context)

1. How long have you been working in [category]?
2. What is your role in [category]? What do you actually do day-to-day?
3. Looking back over the last 5-10 years of your work, what has surprised you the most about [category]?
4. What did you believe when you started that you no longer believe?

### Insight-Hunting Questions (the core)

5. What do you know about [category] that most practitioners get wrong?
6. What pattern do you see across clients (or projects, or customers) that no one in the industry talks about?
7. If you had to teach a new hire one non-obvious thing about [category], what would it be?
8. What is the most expensive mistake you see clients make repeatedly?
9. What is the cheapest insight you have that delivers the most outsized impact?
10. When a client is failing at [category], what are the first three things you check?
11. When a client is winning at [category], what does that pattern look like?
12. What advice do you give that other consultants in [category] do not give?
13. What conventional wisdom in [category] is actually wrong, in your experience?
14. What unconventional approach has worked for you that you cannot easily explain to skeptics?

### Specificity Questions (test depth)

15. Can you walk me through a specific recent client where this insight was the difference between success and failure?
16. What data do you have that supports this insight? Project records, client outcomes, surveys, anything?
17. How would you measure whether someone is correctly applying this insight versus not?
18. Where have you seen this insight fail or not apply?

### Framework-Shape Questions (start naming what you found)

19. If you had to teach this insight in three steps, what would the steps be?
20. If you had to name the pattern you have identified, what would you call it?
21. Are there existing terms in the industry that get close to this but miss in specific ways?
22. What would be a useful checklist a practitioner could use to apply this insight?
23. What are the boundaries or principles or rules that govern this insight?

### Wrap-Up Questions

24. If we were to publish this insight as a framework that the industry could use, what would the most important thing to get right be?
25. Who in the industry would have the strongest reaction to this framework, positive or negative? What would they say?

## Practitioner Validation Questions (Stage 1, External)

After the internal interviews surface a candidate insight, validate it with 5-10 practitioners outside the brand. Aim for 20-30 minute conversations.

Frame the insight in your own words (not the brand's). Do not name it yet. Then ask:

1. Does this pattern match what you see in your work?
2. Have you heard this articulated before? Where?
3. Is this widely known in [category], or is it more niche?
4. If you had to teach this to someone, would you?
5. What would make this more useful to you?
6. Is there a name you would naturally use for this?
7. What are the obvious objections to this insight?
8. Where does it not apply?

What you are listening for:

- **Recognition without prior naming.** "Yes, I see that. I do not think anyone has named it." This is the strongest signal of framework potential.
- **Useful but not novel.** "Yes, that is right, but everyone knows it." Too generic for a framework.
- **Novel but not useful.** "Interesting, but I do not see why I would think about it that way." The framework needs a job to do.
- **Useful and novel.** "Wait, that is a thing? I want to apply that to my work." Strong signal.
- **Disagreement.** "I have not seen that pattern at all." Either the insight is wrong, or the practitioner is not the target audience, or the insight is more niche than expected.

## Stage 2 Naming Test Questions

After the short list of name candidates is built (5-8 options), test with the same external practitioners or a fresh group of 5-10. Frame as a quick informal test, not a market research project.

For each candidate name:

1. What do you think this name describes? (Before any explanation.) The right name lets practitioners guess plausibly close to the actual framework.
2. If you heard this name in a podcast, would you remember it a week later?
3. Would you actually say this name out loud in a meeting? Read it aloud.
4. Does this sound like the kind of name that fits in [category], or does it feel out of place?
5. If you wanted to look this up online, would the name be unique enough that you would find what you are looking for?

After getting reactions, briefly explain what the framework is and ask:

6. Now that you know what it does, does the name still fit?
7. Of the [N] names I showed you, which is the most memorable?
8. Of the [N] names, which is the most credible / serious?

What you are listening for:

- **A name that scores well on both memorability AND credibility.** Some names are catchy but feel unserious; others are credible but forgettable. The best names are both.
- **Practitioner recall.** A week after the test, you can follow up: "What was the name of the framework I showed you?" Names practitioners remember without prompting are strong.
- **Mismatch between guess-from-name and actual framework.** If practitioners guess something very different from what the framework is, the name is misleading or vague.

## Stage 3 Canonical Explainer Review Questions

Before publishing the canonical explainer, review with 3-5 trusted practitioners. Ask:

1. Read just the first paragraph. What is this framework about? (Inverted pyramid test.)
2. Read just the section headings. Can you predict the framework's structure from the headings alone? (Heading-as-question test.)
3. Read the whole page. What is missing that you would want answered?
4. Are the worked examples convincing? Specific enough? Diverse enough?
5. Are the misuse / anti-pattern sections honest? Or do they sound defensive?
6. Would you cite this in your own writing? What would you say when citing it?
7. If you sent this to a colleague, what would they think?

What you are looking for:

- The first-paragraph test passes (someone can grasp the framework from the opening alone).
- The headings tell the story without the body.
- Worked examples cover enough range that practitioners in different niches can see themselves.
- Misuse sections build credibility rather than feel like CYA.
- Reviewer reactions are "I would cite this," not "this is interesting but not citable."

## Ongoing Validation (Months 4-12 of Distribution)

After launch, track:

1. Are external practitioners using the framework name without prompting? (Search the framework name monthly.)
2. Are the practitioners using it correctly, or have they misunderstood?
3. Are there common misinterpretations that need addressing via additional content?
4. Is the framework being cited in podcasts, articles, talks the brand had no hand in?

If practitioners are using the name correctly: keep distributing. The flywheel is engaging.

If practitioners are misusing the name: address with clarifying content, not by renaming. Misuse usually indicates the canonical explainer needs sharpening on the specific point being missed.

If practitioners are not using the name at all after 12 months of distribution: either the framework was wrong, the name was wrong, or distribution was insufficient. Audit each. Killing the framework and starting over is sometimes the right move; never feels good but beats decade-long sunk-cost.

## Trap: The Insight That Sounds Like a Framework But Is Not

Some insights look frameworkable but are not. Patterns to watch for:

- **The insight is really a slogan.** "Customer obsession" is a slogan; it does not have a teachable structure.
- **The insight is a description of work the brand does.** "Our process" is not a framework even if it is well-documented.
- **The insight is a list of tips.** "Five things that help with X" is a listicle, not a framework.
- **The insight is a metric with a goal.** "Aim for 80% retention" is a target, not a framework.

Real frameworks have structure: components, steps, principles, or a way of categorizing that practitioners can apply to new situations they have not seen before. If the candidate insight cannot generate that kind of structure, it is not yet a framework.

If you find this is the case during validation, return to Stage 1. The right insight is somewhere else, deeper in the interviews. Better to discover this before naming and publishing than after.
